Dataset

Source

The dataset used in this project is provided by the Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS), part of the U.S. Department of Transportation (USDOT). Specifically, it originates from the Airline Service Quality Performance (ASQP) database. Under federal regulations air carriers with at least 1% of domestic scheduled-passenger revenues are legally required to report monthly on-time performance and detailed flight delay causes to the BTS.

Licence

As a U.S. federal government dataset, it is released into the public domain (U.S government Data). This framework allows us to download, analyse, modify, and publish results based on the dataset without requiring a license or proprietary permissions.

Structure of the dataset

The dataset is structured as a monthly summary grouped by airline and airport. Each row in the dataset gives information about how a specific carrier performed at a specific airport during a specific month and year.

The 25 variables can be grouped into four categories:

  • Identifiers: Who, where and when (year, month, carrier, carrier_name, airport, airport_name)

  • Headcounts: the raw number of flights that scheduled to land, how many were on time and how many ended up cancelled or diverted (arr_flights, arr_del15, arr_cancelled, arr_diverted, ontime_flights)

  • Responsibility: The number of flights assigned to a specific delay category (carrier_ct, weather_ct, nas_ct, security_ct, late_aircraft_ct)

  • Rates: The actual duration of the delays in minutes and the calculated rates to show the percentage of flights on time vs late (delay_rate, onetime_rate and avg_delay_per_delayed_flight)

Additionally, when a single flight experiences multiple sources of delay, the dataset allocates both the minutes and flight counts proportionally. This ensures that the sum of all fractional count variables within any given row equals the total integer count of delayed flights.

For example, if a flight is delayed 60 minutes, with 30 minutes due to carrier issues and 30 minutes due to weather, both carrier_delay and weather_delay receive 30 minutes, while carrier_ct and weather_ct each receive a fractional count of 0.5.

Research Questions

  1. How do flight delays vary across airlines, airports and over time? What is the distribution of different delay causes across flights, airlines and airports? What temporal and seasonal patterns can be observed?

  2. Is there a relationship between the number of flights (arr_flights) and the number of delayed flights (arr_del15)? Can we predict total arrival delays based on flight volume and delay causes?

Context and Audience

This analysis is conducted in an academic setting as part of the Advanced Statistical Programming course.

The primary target audience is lecturers.

The results are presented as an interactive quarto report that combines code, visualisations and text for data storytelling and reproducible analysis.

Data quality checks

Due to the large size of the dataset, a random sample of 55,000 observations was taken.

  • Variables are correctly typed overall, with month potentially better treated as a categorical variable.

  • In one row (40418), negative values were identified in nas_ct(-0.01) and nas_delay(-1). Both values were treated as missing values (NA).

  • Delay-cause variables such as carrier_ct contain decimal values because they represent allocated delay causes rather than strict counts.

  • Several delay-related variables contain missing values (664–964 observations), which appear to be structurally driven.

  • No obvious inconsistencies were found in categorical variables.

# compute year delay rate over years
yearly <- delay %>%
  group_by(year) %>%
  summarise(
    total_flights = sum(arr_flights, na.rm = TRUE),
    total_delayed = sum(arr_del15, na.rm = TRUE),
    delay_rate = total_delayed / total_flights
  )

# plot the trend
ggplot(yearly, aes(x = year, y = delay_rate)) +
  geom_line(linewidth = 1) +
  geom_point(size = 2) +
