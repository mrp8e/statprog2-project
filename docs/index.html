Introduction
Tip✈️ Flight Delays in the United States

This dashboard explores monthly flight delays across the United States using publicly available airline performance data. Interactive visualisations and statistical analyses are used to investigate delay patterns across airlines, airports, and time.

The dataset contains monthly summaries of airline performance collected by the U.S. federal government. Each observation represents the performance of a specific airline at a specific airport during a given month.

The data includes:

  • Airline and airport identifiers
  • Year and month
  • Number of arriving flights
  • Delayed, cancelled and diverted flights
  • Delay durations by cause
  • On-time performance measures
  • Overall delay rates

Results:

The response variable showed substantial overdispersion (variance > mean), making a quasi-Poisson regression more appropriate than a standard Poisson model.

Security delay had the strongest positive association with the monthly number of delayed flights, followed by the number of arriving flights. Weather delay, NAS delay, and late aircraft delay showed relatively small positive effects, while carrier delay had a slight negative coefficient after controlling for the other predictors.

VIF values ranged from 2.27 to 5.80, indicating low to moderate multicollinearity without evidence of severe collinearity.

The observed-versus-predicted plot shows that the model captures the overall trend but produces larger prediction errors for observations with very high delay counts.

Discussion:

The regression results indicate that operational delay measures are associated with the number of delayed flights, but these relationships should not be interpreted as causal effects because both predictors and the response were derived from the same monthly operational data.

The negative coefficient for carrier delay likely reflects overlap and shared variation with the other delay variables rather than a true negative effect.

Although multicollinearity was present, the VIF values suggest that it is unlikely to substantially affect the overall model. The model explains the general pattern of delayed flights but is less accurate for extreme observations, indicating that additional factors influence unusually high delay counts.
